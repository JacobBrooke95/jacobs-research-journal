Gemma 4 Is Google's Most Interesting Open Model Yet

True Apache 2.0, a first-ever MoE design, audio on edge models, and benchmarks worth taking seriously — but not uncritically.

The thing that caught my eye about Gemma 4 wasn't a benchmark number. It was a license. Apache 2.0 — genuinely, with no asterisks, no carve-outs, no "harmful use" policy clause that gives Google a backdoor to revoke access if you become a competitor. Google had never done this with Gemma before. Every prior version came with a custom Gemma Terms of Use that restricted commercial use above certain thresholds and gave the company broad termination rights. That's gone now. Gemma 4 is, for the first time in this lineage, actually open-source in the way developers mean when they say "actually open-source."

It dropped on April 2nd, 2026, announced by a product manager and a developer relations engineer — a deliberately understated rollout for a release that does a lot of things quietly right. Four models, across two different design philosophies, with a technical depth that rewards reading the architecture paper. Let me walk through what's actually here.

The Four Models

Google is shipping two distinct tiers with Gemma 4. The "E" models (E2B and E4B) are edge-optimized and genuinely meant to run on phones and Raspberry Pis. The larger models (26B and 31B) are for workstations, servers, and GPU clusters.

The naming is slightly confusing. E2B has 5.1 billion total parameters when you include its embedding tables, but only about 2.3 billion effective compute parameters during inference. E4B sits at 8B total, 4.5B effective. Both have 128K context windows. The 26B is a Mixture of Experts model — 25.2B total parameters but only 3.8B active at any moment — with a 256K context window. The 31B is a traditional dense model at full 30.7B parameters, also with 256K context.

E2B (~5 GB), E4B (~8 GB), 26B MoE (~18 GB), 31B Dense (~20 GB) — all weights, all free to use commercially, no custom license.

What Changed Architecturally

Gemma 3 was a straightforward dense model. Gemma 4 introduces several meaningful departures.

The biggest is the Mixture of Experts design in the 26B model — a first for the Gemma lineage. MoE architectures route each token through a subset of "expert" sub-networks rather than the whole model. The 26B has 128 experts total; 8 are activated per token at inference time. The promise: you're paying roughly 4B-parameter inference compute while theoretically drawing on 26B-parameter-worth of learned representations. The reality is a bit messier, which I'll get to.

The edge models have something called Per-Layer Embeddings. There's a second embedding table that injects a small learned residual into every decoder layer, helping the model maintain token identity through the full depth of the network. It's why E2B and E4B punch harder than 2-4 billion parameters would normally suggest. The extra embedding parameters inflate the model's file size without adding meaningfully to inference cost.

All models use alternating attention layers: sliding-window local attention (512-token window for small models, 1,024 for large) interleaved with global full-context attention. Two different rotary position embeddings handle each layer type. This design isn't just clever — it's explicitly optimized to be quantization-friendly and to work cleanly with the inference libraries that developers actually use.

There's also a shared KV cache on the final N layers that reuses key-value tensors from earlier in the forward pass, cutting memory pressure for long-context inference. On paper, thoughtful engineering. In practice, the community has found that memory at 256K context is still brutal — but we'll get there.

Multimodal From the Ground Up

This is the part of Gemma 4 that I find genuinely exciting. The multimodal architecture here isn't a patch or a post-hoc adapter — it's built into the model design from the beginning.

The vision encoder uses learned 2D positional encodings and multidimensional RoPE, preserving original image aspect ratios rather than squashing everything to a square. You can configure the token budget per image: 70, 140, 280, 560, or 1,120 tokens, trading inference speed against visual detail. For a chart in a document you're parsing, 70 tokens might be plenty. For detailed object detection in a high-res photo, you'd go to 560 or 1,120. That's a genuinely useful design choice — it gives developers a knob rather than a fixed trade-off.

The audio encoder is the other thing that stands out. The E2B and E4B edge models support text + image + audio + video with audio. That's a lot for a model you can run on a phone. The audio encoder is a USM-style conformer that Google compressed from 681 million parameters (in Gemma 3n) down to 305 million — a 55% reduction — while maintaining quality. Demonstrated capabilities include real-time speech transcription, speech-to-translated-text, and Q&A over audio recordings. The 26B and 31B models skip audio support entirely, handling images and video (without audio) only.

All four models support multimodal function calling — trained in from scratch, not the usual hack of instruction-following a model that wasn't designed for it. And multimodal reasoning tokens work too: you can turn on extended thinking mode while feeding the model an image or a video frame.

The Benchmarks, Honestly

The 31B Dense model lands roughly at third place on the LMArena text leaderboard. The 26B MoE is around sixth. On paper, this is impressive.

The specific numbers are strong where Gemma 4 wants to be strong. On MMLU Pro, the 31B scores 85.2%. On AIME 2026 math reasoning, it hits 89.2%. On LiveCodeBench v6 coding, 80.0%. The Codeforces ELO estimate of 2150 for the 31B is genuinely elite. If you care specifically about code generation, Gemma 4 competes.

But the honest competitive read is more complicated. When you put Gemma 4 31B next to Qwen 3.5 27B — a model from a Chinese lab that's been setting the pace in open weights — Qwen wins on MMLU Pro (86.1% vs. 85.2%) and wins more meaningfully on Humanity's Last Exam reasoning (24.3% vs. 19.5%). Gemma 4 beats Qwen on coding (2150 vs. 1899 Codeforces ELO). They're close on human preference. One community commenter's blunt read: Gemma 4 "ties with Qwen, if not Qwen being slightly ahead. And Qwen 3.5 is more compute efficient too."

"Architecture-wise, besides multimodal support, Gemma 4 (31B) looks pretty much unchanged compared to Gemma 3 (27B)."

That's ML researcher Sebastian Raschka on X. I think his point is fair. The gains in the dense 31B path appear to come from training data and recipe improvements — which is valid and real — but not from radical architectural innovation. The architectural novelty in Gemma 4 is concentrated in the MoE design and the multimodal pipeline.

Against closed models: Gemma 4 doesn't challenge Claude 3.7 or GPT-4.5 on complex multi-step reasoning. But that's not really the comparison that matters for most people running open models.

Running It Locally

The honest VRAM story is more demanding than you'd hope from "4B active parameters."

The E4B runs comfortably on an RTX 3060 or an M1 Mac with 16GB unified memory. The E2B will run on almost anything with a GPU — including phones, per Google's demo. For most indie developers and researchers who want to experiment, these two are the entry points.

The 26B MoE is where things get complicated. In BF16, you need roughly 52 GB VRAM — that's a multi-GPU setup. At Q4 quantization, it comes down to around 14 GB, which fits in a single RTX 4090. But MoE memory overhead at inference time is higher than the "3.8B active parameters" framing suggests. Community reports on HuggingFace and the Unsloth Discord flagged that at long context windows, the 26B spikes well beyond its base weights. The 256K context window is theoretically available; actually using it on consumer hardware is another matter.

The 31B Dense is cleaner. No routing overhead, predictable memory. At Q4 quantization it's around 17 GB, which fits on an RTX 4090 or an M2/M3 Max Mac with 24+ GB unified memory. If you have that hardware, this is the model to run.

Via Ollama, the path is straightforward: 

ollama pull gemma4:e4b    # good starting point, ~8GB
ollama pull gemma4:26b    # MoE, ~18GB Q4
ollama pull gemma4:31b    # dense, ~20GB Q4
ollama run gemma4:e4b

The thinking mode is worth enabling for any non-trivial reasoning task. Via HuggingFace's API it's enable_thinking=True; the underlying mechanism is a <|think|> token that triggers up to 4,000 reasoning tokens before the final response. In practice this meaningfully changes output quality on math and coding tasks.

What This Actually Changes

Let me say clearly what I think is new here and what isn't.

The Apache 2.0 license is a big deal that's getting underreported. Google has historically been cagey about open-weights licensing — every carve-out and custom policy is a claim on the model's commercial value and a way to avoid funding competitors. Dropping that entirely signals either genuine confidence that these weights aren't a competitive threat to Gemini, or a strategic shift toward winning developer mindshare. Maybe both. Either way, for anyone building products with Gemma 4, the licensing risk that existed before is gone.

The multimodal design — especially the edge-model audio support and the configurable vision token budget — is legitimately differentiated in the open-weights space. As of this writing, there are not many open models you can run locally that handle text + image + audio + video with audio at the E4B parameter count. That's a meaningful capability for anyone building mobile or edge applications.

What it doesn't change: the competitive picture in open-weights benchmarks. Qwen continues to push hard, DeepSeek is still a real force, and Google is competitive but not dominant. The "open AI" race has several strong players and Gemma 4 holds a respectable position in it — third or sixth depending on the leaderboard — but if you were hoping for a clear winner, this isn't that release.

For most people reading about AI model releases: the E4B is the model to download first. It's small enough to run on a laptop, capable enough to be useful, genuinely multimodal including audio, and free under a real open-source license. That's a combination that didn't exist six months ago. That's the story.
