The Word That Explains Government

By Jacob Brooke

Subscribe to podcast →

In March 1944, the chairman of a congressional committee issued a memo to his staff at the U.S. Smaller War Plants Corporation. The memo was terse. It said, "Anyone using the words 'activation' or 'implementation' will be shot." His name was Maury Maverick, a Texas congressman. He was done.

The thing Maverick was done with was the language his own government used to talk to itself — the endless, self-important, impenetrable sentences that made simple ideas sound important and made important ideas sound like accident reports. He invented a word for it that day: gobbledegook.

The Origin of a Perfect Word

Maverick said he'd made the word up "in imitation of turkey noise." The specific image was a turkey "always gobbledy gobbling and strutting with ludicrous pomposity." If you've spent five minutes in a government office, you know exactly what he meant. The word arrived fully formed, already perfect.

By May 1944, Maverick had taken his complaint public in the New York Times Magazine. The response wasn't a wave of reform — it was the early version of what we'd eventually call a meme. Gobbledegook spread. It was short, it was funny, it was exactly right. Within years it had made the dictionaries. By the 1960s, the government was so tired of hearing the word that an official style manual was titled "Gobbledygook Has Gotta Go."

Eighty-two years later, I work in state government in Missouri. Every day I read and write documents that are pure Maverick. And nothing has changed.

What Gobbledegook Actually Is

It's not just bureaucratic language. It's not even jargon — jargon is specialized language that experts use with each other, which is sometimes necessary and almost always defensible.

Gobbledegook is the performative use of complexity to do three things simultaneously: make yourself sound important, obscure responsibility, and create a barrier to understanding that keeps power concentrated.

Maverick's own definition: "talk or writing which is long, pompous, vague, involved, usually with Latinized words." The classic tell is passive voice. "It has been determined that..." instead of "I determined that..." or "We decided that..." Remove the door-opener from the sentence, and nobody knows who did the deciding.

Then there's the pseudo-precision. In government you'll see phrases like "operationalize deliverables" or "leverage synergistic metrics" — words that sound technical enough to resist questioning, but say nothing specific. They're a force field around a void.

Why It Persists

The frustrating part is that we know how to fix it. Congress passed the Plain Writing Act in 2010. It mandated that federal agencies write in clear language. The guidance is straightforward: use active voice, use short words, tell people what you're doing.

And yet. Despite eighty years of complaint, modern estimates suggest only about 10% of government material meets clear language standards. The Department of Defense still publishes budget justifications that require a PhD to parse. State government — my world — is somehow even worse, because we're smaller, older, and we still have people who genuinely believe that government language has to be incomprehensible to be legitimate.

There's a psychological component. Government workers revert to industry jargon even in documents written for average citizens partly because there's a feeling that if it doesn't sound governmental, it can't be official. The complexity is the credential. The unreadability is the badge.

And there's a structural component: by writing in gobbledegook, you make it harder to hold anyone accountable. If nobody understands what was decided or who decided it, nobody can easily challenge the decision. The opacity is protective.

The Personal Angle

I think about this because I write policy memos. When I started this job, I wrote them the way I'd been trained to write them — in the idiom of Missouri state government, which is to say, in code. Long sentences. Jargon substituted for clarity. Passive voice where active would have been better. I was fluent in gobbledegook and proud of it.

Then I realized something: the people reading my memos were usually trying to understand what I actually meant. They weren't impressed by the complexity. They were just frustrated. And if they were frustrated, then citizens reading the same policies — if they could find them, if they could understand them — would be even more frustrated.

The turning point was writing a budget memo that my director sent back with a note: "Can you say this in English?" I could. I rewrote it in 200 words instead of 400. Added a summary in bullet points. Used the word "I" and "we" instead of the passive fog.

It didn't feel official at first. It felt plain. But it was clearer, faster to read, and actually more persuasive — because you could understand what I was actually proposing instead of getting lost in the language.

What Clear Language Actually Does

This matters more than it seems. Clear government language isn't about tone. It's about power.

When a budget document is written clearly, citizens can actually understand what their government is spending money on. When a regulation is written clearly, businesses can actually comply with it instead of guessing. When a policy memo uses active voice and specific language, bureaucrats can actually be held accountable for decisions instead of hiding behind the passive "it has been determined."

Maury Maverick understood this in 1944. That memo wasn't a stylistic preference. It was a statement that government owes its citizens clarity. That's not a nice-to-have. That's foundational. You can't have accountability without transparency, and you can't have transparency when the language itself is a wall.

The word "gobbledegook" has been around for eighty-two years. The Plain Writing Act is sixteen years old. And yet I still read government documents that sound like they were written by committee to obscure rather than explain.

The thing Maverick understood — the thing we still need to understand — is that clear writing is a political act. It's a claim that what the government does matters enough to explain properly, and that citizens matter enough to deserve clarity.

Everything else is just gobbledegook.
